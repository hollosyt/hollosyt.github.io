Birds of Prey

248 detections 7 species avg conf: 0.83
2025: 202 2026: 46

Seven raptor species were detected across the monitoring period, with the Red-tailed Hawk dominating detections as expected for a year-round resident of open Michigan farmland. Cooper's Hawk and Merlin showed clear spring and fall spikes corresponding to their migration windows, while Bald Eagle and Osprey detections clustered around early spring as these fish-hunters return to the region. American Kestrel, once a common Michigan farmland bird, appeared only sparingly — consistent with their documented long-term population decline in the Midwest. The relatively low overall count (244) reflects both the naturally lower density of apex predators and the fact that acoustic detection of raptors is inherently harder than for vocal songbirds.

Owls

601 detections 2 species avg conf: 0.89
2025: 596 2026: 5

Only two owl species registered in the dataset — Barred Owl and Great Horned Owl — both year-round Michigan residents. All 596 detections fell within 2025, with a notable absence in 2026, suggesting the sensor may not have been running overnight by early 2026 or that detection conditions changed seasonally. Great Horned Owls are among the earliest nesters in North America, often calling from January onward, and any winter detections here would be especially noteworthy. Barred Owls are increasingly common in suburban woodland edges and their characteristic "who cooks for you" call is reliably picked up by acoustic monitors at night.

Woodpeckers

12,995 detections 7 species avg conf: 0.82
2025: 9,502 2026: 3,493

With over 12,600 detections across seven species, woodpeckers were one of the most consistently present groups throughout the year. Red-bellied Woodpecker led the count, followed closely by Downy Woodpecker — both year-round fixtures at feeding stations and in forest edges. Northern Flicker showed strong spring activity as migrants and local breeders become vocal. Yellow-bellied Sapsucker appears seasonally, drilling neat rows of sap wells in birches and maples during migration. Pileated Woodpecker, the largest North American woodpecker and the inspiration for Woody Woodpecker, contributed a small but exciting set of detections. Red-headed Woodpecker, a species of conservation concern in Michigan due to habitat loss, registered a handful of detections — each one worth noting.

Ducks

8,097 detections 9 species avg conf: 0.82
2025: 6,844 2026: 1,253

Ten duck species were recorded across the monitoring period, with Mallard and Wood Duck dominating as the most abundant and year-round familiar faces. The strong March and April peaks align with spring migration, when puddle ducks move through central Michigan in large numbers. Blue-winged Teal is one of the earliest departing fall migrants among dabbling ducks, explaining its late-summer presence. Ring-necked Duck and Common Goldeneye are diving ducks that favor deeper water; their detections likely reflect brief flyovers or nearby wetland use. Long-tailed Duck is an uncommon inland detection, likely a migrant pushing through during a weather event. The strong 2025 counts versus limited 2026 data reflect the partial year of 2026 monitoring.

Waterfowl (Geese & Swans)

6,442 detections 5 species avg conf: 0.83
2025: 4,500 2026: 1,942

Canada Goose dominated this group as expected — a year-round resident that has become ubiquitous across Michigan's parks, golf courses, and wetlands. The spring and fall migration pulses are clearly visible in the monthly data, when noisy V-formations are a daily sight and sound overhead. Snow Goose detections are a treat, typically occurring during spring migration as flocks push north — occasionally in the thousands. Trumpeter Swan, once extirpated from Michigan and now successfully reintroduced, and Tundra Swan, a true migrant passing through in spring and fall, both contributed detections. Greater White-fronted Goose rounds out the group as an occasional migrant through the Great Lakes region.

Songbirds & Passerines

112,813 detections 88 species avg conf: 0.81
2025: 94,754 2026: 18,059

The backbone of the dataset with over 110,000 detections across 87 species, this group spans everything from wintering finches to neotropical warblers. House Finch was the single most detected species in the entire database — a testament to their year-round feeder presence and loud, burbling song. Eastern Wood-Pewee's massive count of 17,450 is striking and suggests a breeding territory very close to the sensor, as this flycatcher sings persistently from a fixed perch all summer. The warbler diversity in May is spectacular, with Cape May, Magnolia, Blackburnian, and Blackpoll all moving through in a matter of weeks. Dark-eyed Junco and White-throated Sparrow bookend the year as winter visitors, arriving in October and departing in April. Species diversity peaks sharply in May during peak migration and again in late summer as resident breeders are still active.

Shorebirds & Waders

2,506 detections 10 species avg conf: 0.87
2025: 1,793 2026: 713

Nine species of shorebirds and wading birds were detected, led by Great Blue Heron — a common year-round presence along waterways. Killdeer is an early spring returnee, often detected on bare ground and agricultural fields before most migrants arrive. Greater Yellowlegs passes through in both spring and fall, often calling loudly in flight. American Woodcock is an early spring migrant whose "peent" display calls make it particularly detectable acoustically at dusk. Sandhill Crane has rebounded impressively in Michigan over the past few decades; their loud, rattling calls carry great distances and are reliably captured. Caspian Tern and Herring Gull reflect proximity to open water or the Great Lakes corridor, and Ring-billed Gull is a year-round fixture in urban Michigan.

Hummingbirds

6 detections 1 species avg conf: 0.76
2025: 6 2026: 0

Just six Ruby-throated Hummingbird detections were recorded, all in 2025. This is the only hummingbird species that breeds in eastern North America, and it typically arrives in Michigan in mid-May, departing by early October. The low detection count likely reflects both the sensor's placement and acoustic detection limits — hummingbirds are more reliably detected visually than by sound, as their vocalizations are quiet and high-pitched. Any detection is a confirmation of their presence in the area, and the timing of these six records would be worth examining closely against the known May–September window.

Corvids

5,014 detections 2 species avg conf: 0.80
2025: 1,767 2026: 3,247

American Crow accounts for all corvid detections — 4,974 in total — making it one of the most detected species overall. Crows are highly vocal, socially complex, and present year-round, which explains their strong showing. What's notable is the dramatic year-over-year shift: 1,743 detections in 2025 but 3,231 in just the first three months of 2026 alone, suggesting either a significant increase in local crow activity, a change in sensor conditions, or the emergence of a roost nearby. Large winter crow roosts are common in Michigan cities and towns, sometimes numbering in the tens of thousands. The January–March 2026 spike deserves further investigation. Blue Jay was categorized under songbirds given its behavioral overlap with passerines at feeders, despite its taxonomic corvid family membership.

Introduced & Invasive

1,131 detections 2 species avg conf: 0.79
2025: 8 2026: 1,123

European Starling and House Sparrow, both Old World introductions now firmly embedded in North American urban and agricultural ecosystems, make up this group. The near-complete absence in 2025 (only 8 detections) versus 947 in early 2026 is striking and almost certainly reflects a change in sensor configuration, placement, or detection thresholds rather than a genuine population change — starlings and sparrows are omnipresent in Michigan. Both species are prolific breeders and cavity competitors that have displaced many native cavity-nesting species, making their monitoring ecologically relevant. The sensor data, once normalized, could provide useful baseline information about their seasonal activity patterns.
